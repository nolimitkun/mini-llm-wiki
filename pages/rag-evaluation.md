# Evaluating RAG systems

A [RAG](rag-overview.md) system has two failure surfaces — retrieval and generation — and they must be **measured separately**, or you'll tune the wrong one. This page covers RAG-specific evaluation; general practice lives in [LLMOps evaluation](evaluation.md).

## The golden dataset

Everything starts with an evaluation set: **50–200 examples** of `(question, expected answer, source document(s))`, built from:

- Real user queries (mine your logs — best source by far),
- Domain-expert-written questions per document category,
- Synthetic questions generated by an LLM from corpus documents (cheap volume; have humans spot-check).

Include hard cases deliberately: multi-hop questions, questions whose answer *isn't* in the corpus (tests refusal), ambiguous phrasing, and questions about tables/numbers.

## Retrieval metrics (component level)

Given labeled relevant chunks/documents per question:

| Metric | Measures |
|---|---|
| **Recall@k** | Is the needed content in the top k? The metric that matters most — generation can't recover from recall failure |
| **MRR / NDCG** | How high does relevant content rank? |
| **Precision@k** | How much of the top k is noise (distracts the generator, wastes tokens)? |

Measure at two points: candidate generation (recall@50–100) and post-rerank (recall@k for the k you actually send) — see [retrieval techniques](retrieval-techniques.md).

## Generation metrics (end to end)

The **RAG triad**, typically scored by an LLM judge:

1. **Faithfulness / groundedness** — is every claim in the answer supported by the retrieved context? (The hallucination metric.)
2. **Answer relevance** — does it actually address the question?
3. **Context relevance** — was the retrieved context pertinent? (Bridges back to retrieval.)

Plus: **correctness** vs. the golden answer, **citation accuracy** (do cited chunks support the cited claims?), and **refusal correctness** (says "I don't know" when the corpus lacks the answer — and *doesn't* over-refuse when it has it).

## LLM-as-judge, used carefully

Judges make RAG evaluation scalable but need their own QA: use a strong model, one criterion per judgment, few-shot rubric examples, and **calibrate against a human-labeled sample** (aim for ≥ 80–90% agreement before trusting trends). Watch for self-preference bias when the judge and generator are the same model family.

## Operating loop

1. **Baseline** the full pipeline on the golden set.
2. **Change one variable** ([chunking](chunking-strategies.md), embedding model, k, reranker, prompt) and re-run — treat it like CI; block regressions.
3. **In production**: sample real traffic for judge scoring, track thumbs-up/down, and mine failures back into the golden set (it should grow monthly).
4. **Re-baseline on model upgrades** — a new generator or embedding model shifts everything.

## Common evaluation mistakes

- Only measuring end-to-end → can't tell retrieval failures from generation failures.
- Synthetic-only eval sets → grade well, fail on real queries' messiness.
- No "unanswerable" cases → refusal behavior untested until a user finds out.
- One-off evaluation → quality drifts with corpus growth and model updates; make it continuous.
